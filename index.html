Summary of Laws

What Each Law Does

RA 9710

Magna Carta of Women

What it does

Comprehensive law guaranteeing equal rights for Filipino women in all areas of life.

Protects

All Filipino women

Why it matters

Eliminates systemic gender discrimination and ensures women's full participation in society.

RA 6725

Anti-Discrimination in Employment

What it does

Prohibits employers from discriminating based on gender in hiring, promotion, and pay.

Protects

Women workers in all employment sectors

Why it matters

Ensures equal opportunity and pay for work of equal value.

RA 7192

Women in Development Act

What it does

Ensures women's equal access to resources, education, credit, and government services.

Protects

Filipino women, especially in rural areas

Why it matters

Promotes women's economic empowerment and equitable development.

RA 7877

Anti-Sexual Harassment Act

What it does

Defines and penalizes sexual harassment in work, education, and training environments.

Protects

Employees, students, and trainees (any gender)

Why it matters

Creates safe spaces and holds perpetrators accountable for abuse of power.

RA 7600

Breastfeeding Act

What it does

Promotes and protects breastfeeding rights of mothers in workplaces and health facilities.

Protects

Mothers and infants nationwide

Why it matters

Supports child nutrition, maternal health, and work-life balance for new mothers.

RA 7322

Maternity Leave Provision

What it does

Grants female employees paid maternity leave for childbirth and miscarriage.

Protects

Female SSS members and government employees

Why it matters

Protects maternal and infant health while securing women's employment rights.

RA 9262

Anti-VAWC Law

What it does

Criminalizes physical, psychological, sexual, and economic abuse against women and children.

Protects

Women and their children in intimate relationships

Why it matters

Provides protection orders and legal remedies against domestic and intimate partner violence.

RA 8353

Anti-Rape Law

What it does

Reclassifies rape as a crime against persons, broadens definition, and increases penalties.

Protects

Rape survivors of any gender

Why it matters

Recognizes rape as a serious human rights violation and strengthens survivor protection.

RA 7822

Anti-Trafficking in Persons Act

What it does

Criminalizes the recruitment, transport, and exploitation of persons for labor or sexual purposes.

Protects

Trafficking victims, especially women and children

Why it matters

Addresses modern-day slavery and protects vulnerable Filipinos from exploitation.

RA 7688

Women in Military Service

What it does

Allows women to serve in the Armed Forces in non-combat and combat roles.

Protects

Filipino women aspiring to serve in the military

Why it matters

Breaks gender barriers in national defense and promotes equal opportunity in public service.

RA 10354

Reproductive Health Law

What it does

Mandates universal access to contraception, reproductive health education, and maternal care.

Protects

All Filipinos, especially women of reproductive age

Why it matters

Upholds reproductive rights and reduces maternal mortality through informed family planning.

SOGIE Bill

Proposed Anti-Discrimination Bill

What it does

Seeks to prohibit discrimination based on sexual orientation, gender identity, and expression.

Protects

LGBTQIA+ individuals and the broader public

Why it matters

Addresses systemic discrimination and promotes inclusive rights for marginalized communities.

Real-Life Application

Scenarios in Practice

Scenario 01

Workplace Harassment & Discrimination

The Situation

Maria, a nurse, was denied a promotion despite being the most qualified. Her male supervisor repeatedly makes inappropriate comments about her appearance and brushes off complaints.

Laws Applied

  • RA 7877 — supervisor's conduct constitutes harassment
  • RA 6725 — denial of promotion based on gender
  • RA 9710 — violation of equal rights in workplace
Maria may file a complaint with DOLE or the Civil Service Commission. The supervisor faces administrative and criminal liability.
Scenario 02

Domestic Violence & VAWC

The Situation

Ana and her children are subjected to physical and emotional abuse by her husband. He controls her finances, isolates her from family, and threatens harm if she tries to leave.

Laws Applied

  • RA 9262 — physical, psychological, and economic abuse
  • RA 9710 — right to security and freedom from violence
Ana can obtain a Barangay Protection Order (BPO) within 24 hours. A Temporary Protection Order (TPO) can be issued by the court, and the husband may face imprisonment.
Scenario 03

Human Trafficking & Exploitation

The Situation

Lina, 17, was lured by a recruiter promising overseas work. She was brought to another city and forced into commercial sexual exploitation, unable to leave or contact her family.

Laws Applied

  • RA 7822 — recruitment and exploitation of a minor
  • RA 9262 — violence and coercion
  • RA 9710 — right to protection from exploitation
Traffickers face life imprisonment. Lina is entitled to full support including shelter, counseling, and legal assistance from the government.
Scenario 04

LGBTQIA+ Discrimination (SOGIE)

The Situation

Carlos, a gay employee, was fired when his employer discovered his sexual orientation. He was also denied rental housing and faced public ridicule with no legal recourse.

Laws Applied

  • SOGIE Bill (Proposed) — would prohibit this type of discrimination
  • Current Gap: No national SOGIE law exists
  • Local ordinances in some LGUs may offer partial protection
This scenario underscores the urgent need to pass the SOGIE Bill nationally. Without it, LGBTQIA+ Filipinos remain vulnerable to discrimination.

Critical Reflection

How These Laws Promote Gender Equality

01

Legal Recognition of Rights

Laws like RA 9710 and RA 10354 formally establish that women have the same rights as men in education, employment, healthcare, and government participation — transforming social norms into enforceable standards.

02

Protection from Gender-Based Violence

RA 9262, RA 8353, and RA 7822 criminalize violence, rape, and trafficking. These laws send a clear message that gender-based harm is not acceptable and that survivors deserve justice and support.

03

Economic & Career Empowerment

RA 6725, RA 7192, RA 7322, and RA 7688 remove barriers that kept women out of workplaces, military service, and financial systems — enabling economic self-sufficiency and professional growth.

04

Safe & Inclusive Spaces

RA 7877 and RA 7600 protect women in environments like offices and hospitals. The proposed SOGIE Bill would extend these protections to LGBTQIA+ individuals, fostering a society where all genders feel safe.

Critical Reflection

Issues That Still Exist

Low Reporting & Impunity

Many victims of sexual harassment, rape, and VAWC do not report due to shame, fear of retaliation, or distrust in authorities. Perpetrators often go unpunished, undermining the deterrent effect of the law.

SOGIE Bill Not Yet Passed

Despite decades of advocacy, the SOGIE Bill remains unenacted. LGBTQIA+ Filipinos continue to face discrimination in employment, housing, and public life with minimal legal recourse at the national level.

Weak Implementation

Many laws lack the budget, trained personnel, or political will for proper enforcement. Protection orders under RA 9262 are sometimes ignored; traffickers evade justice; harassers face minimal consequences.

Persistent Cultural Barriers

Patriarchal norms, victim-blaming, and gender stereotypes limit the effectiveness of laws. Women in rural areas and indigenous communities often have limited awareness of or access to legal protections.

Reproductive Health Gaps

RA 10354 faces resistance from religious groups and local officials who block access to contraceptives in some areas, leaving many women — especially the poor — without the family planning services they are legally entitled to.

Trafficking Remains Rampant

Despite RA 7822, the Philippines remains a major source country for trafficking victims. Online recruitment, corruption among officials, and poverty continue to fuel exploitation, especially of women and children.
